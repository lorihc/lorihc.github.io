Chicken or Egg?

So I'm watching Crossfire (why I do this to myself, I don't know—I've become a CNN junkie lately), and Paul Begala was just pointing out that the areas most affected by terrorism—namely, New York City and its suburbs, and Arlington County, Virginia, home of the Pentagon—voted overwhelmingly Democratic on November 2. The suggestion was that those people who had experienced the most horrific terrorism did not support President Bush's plan to fight it.

word cd-key

Tucker Carlson called this argument "stupid," and responded that urban centers vote Democratic, and terrorists naturally strike urban centers, because that's where the people are. While that may be true, I still wonder why those least likely to be affected by terrorism seem to have the most faith in Bush's ability to fight it, while those most affected by terrorism could not be swayed away from their Democratic leanings? Perhaps the middle-state Bush supporters are thinking, "well, my subdivision hasn't been attacked yet, so he must be doing a great job!"

If this sounds patronizing, consider that in several interviews with Bush supporters I've read over the past year (and one I conducted with a member of my own family this week), the phrase "better to fight them over there than over here" came up. (The scary thing is that this phrase, in every case, was in reference to the war in Iraq, which some people still seem to confuse with the war on terror. Iraq has never attacked us; Saddam didn't have the capability, even if he had the will.)

In any case, I'm puzzled. Why are people in the middle of the country so scared?
